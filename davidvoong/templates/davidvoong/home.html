Data Science

Distributed Systems

With the rapid growth of data in current times comes the opportunity for insight into many aspects of life. In order to extract information our data analysis methods need to grow in terms of efficiency and speed. Distributed and cloud computing allow us to leverage our resources to achieve this. I use technologies such the LHC Computing Grid, the Hadoop ecosystem and cloud services such as IBM's Bluemix, Microsoft's Azure and Google's Cloud Platform.

Statistics

At the core of any data science is statistics. Statistical methods allow large data sets to be summarised in a small set of numerical values or visualisations e.g. in a graph or histogram. They also give us an understanding on the limitations of our data, what are the errors on our data like? How do they vary with the size of our dataset? And to what degree of belief in our calculations can we have? My background in Physics gives me a solid foundation on which to tackle these questions.

Visualisation

Data visualisation is a key tool for any data scientist, it allows data to be represented in a more intuitive way, helping people to make better data driven decisions. I use libraries such as pandas (with matplotlib) and the particle physics favourite ROOT for data exploration. In addition to this for more interactive data visualisations in the context of the web such as interactive maps, data networks and animated charts I use the d3 library.

Data Modelling

Modelling your data allows you to make predictions. This involves fitting your data to a model using statistical methods such as a Least Squares or Maximum Likelihood method. Your model and its fit can then be validated using a Chi Squared test whilst the error of the model can be calculated from the Covariance matrix of the model parameters from the fit. I use the numpy and scipy python libraries for this as well as the Particle Physics library Minuit2.

Databases

Databases are used to store data, they come in all shapes and sizes depending on whether you require high read, write, creation, deletion speeds or consistent, secure, scalable and robust data storage. Having a deep understanding of the what the right tool for the job is essential when developing your system. I have experience with relational databases such as MySQL, PostgreSQL as well as noSQL JSON document orientated databases such as MongoDB.

Education

PhD Particle Physics

Particle Physics is the study of the fundamental constituents of energy and matter. At the LHCb detector - one of the detectors in the Large Hadron Collider at CERN - I researched particle production rates. Particle production occurs when the interaction between two particles is so great that particle anti-particle pairs are spontaneously created from the vacuum, resulting in events hundreds of particles. This process is dominated by QCD physics, a theory notorious for being difficult in terms of mathematical calculations. Better understanding in this process allows physicists to get a better picture of very rare processes which may be obscured by the myriad of particles.

MSc Physics

At UCL I worked on Rivet a toolkit for validation of Monte Carlo event generators. Monte Carlo event generators are used to simulate particle physics events and use random number generators to simulate the innate randomness in quantum mechanical processes. Rivet allows experimental physicists a systematic way in which to make comparisons between real data and Monte Carlo simulated data helping generator developers to fine tune the parameters in their models. Monte Carlo generators are important in giving insight into physics processes as well as how future experiments should be built to maximise sensitivity to certain processes.

BSc Physics with Space Science

At the University of Leicester my research project involved simulating the growth of surfaces. I developed computational models based on theoretical papers on surface growth. Also there were fractals which are cool.
