Data Science

Distributed Systems

With the rapid growth of data in current times comes the opportunity for insight into many aspects of our environment. In order to extract information our data analysis methods need to grow in terms of efficiency and speed, techniques such as parallelisation, distributed and cloud computing allow us to leverage our resources to achieve this. I use technologies such the LHC Computing Grid, the Hadoop ecosystem and cloud services such as IBM's Bluemix, Microsoft's Azure and Google's Cloud Platform.

Statistics

At the core of any data science is statistics. Statistical methods allow large data sets to be summarised in a small set of numerical values or visualisations e.g. in a graph or histogram. They also give us an understanding on the limitations of our data, what are the errors on our data like? How do they vary with the size of our dataset? And to what degree of belief in our calculations can we have? My background in Physics gives me a solid foundation on which to tackle these questions.

Visualisation

Data visualisation is a key tool for any data scientist, it allows data to be represented in a more intuitive way, helping people to make better data driven decisions. I use libraries such as matplotlib and the particle physics favourite, ROOT for data exploration. In addition to this, with the growth of the web comes the demand for more interactive data visualisations such as interactive maps, data networks and animated charts. For this sort of web base visualisation I use the d3 library.

Data Modelling

Modelling your data allows you to make predictions. This involves fitting your data to a model using statistical methods such as a Least Squares or Maximum Likelihood method. Your model and its fit can then be validated using a Chi Squared test whilst the error of the model can be calculated from the Covariance matrix of the model parameters from the fit. I use the numpy and scipy python libraries for this as well as the Particle Physics library Minuit2.

Communication

Understanding you data is great but doesn't mean anything if you can't communicate how you analysed your data and what the results mean. A data scientists needs to be able to communicate on many levels, from the non-specialists, junior members, peers and to specialists. Having an academic background which focuses on outreach with the general public, tutoring and high level research gives me experience across this spectrum.
